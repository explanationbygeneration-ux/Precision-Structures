Be Prepared

What to Include in Your Bid Request

Building Dimensions

Describe overall building width, length, and wall height. These measurements help us size your trusses correctly and plan for bearing conditions.

Truss Specifications

Span, pitch, overhang, spacing (typically 24" OC), and heel height. If you have engineered drawings, include those as well.

Load Requirements

Snow load zone, special wind ratings, any concentrated loads (HVAC units, solar panels, etc.). Local building codes determine minimum loads.

Project Timeline

When you need trusses delivered, project start date, any phasing requirements. Early planning ensures we can meet your schedule.

Know Your Trusses

Truss Terminology

  • Top Chord The inclined (or horizontal) upper member of the truss, carrying compressive forces and bending loads from the roof deck down to the bearing points.
  • Bottom Chord The lower horizontal member that ties the truss together, primarily resisting tension forces and supporting ceiling loads.
  • Web Members Interior diagonal and vertical members that form the triangulated pattern, transferring loads between the top and bottom chords.
  • King Post The central vertical web member that connects the apex of the top chord to the midpoint of the bottom chord.
  • Bearing The point where the truss rests on the wall or support structure. Loads transfer through these contact points to the building frame.
  • Heel The junction where the top chord meets the bottom chord at the bearing point. Heel height affects insulation depth at the eave.
  • Span The horizontal distance between the outside edges of the two bearing points — effectively the width the truss covers.
  • Pitch The slope of the roof expressed as rise over run (e.g., 4/12 means 4 inches of rise for every 12 inches of horizontal run).
  • Connector Plate Galvanized steel plates with pre-punched teeth that are pressed into wood at each joint, holding truss members together.

Truss Profiles

Common Truss Types

Each truss type is designed for specific structural and architectural needs.

Fink Truss

The most common residential truss. W-shaped web pattern provides excellent load distribution.

Howe Truss

Vertical webs in compression, diagonal webs in tension. Good for moderate spans.

Pratt Truss

Opposite of Howe — diagonal webs in compression. Used in longer spans.

Scissor Truss

Creates a vaulted ceiling effect. Bottom chord slopes upward toward the center.

Attic Truss

Designed with a usable room space within the truss. Great for bonus rooms.

Mono Truss

Single slope, like half a triangle. Used for additions, porches, and shed roofs.
